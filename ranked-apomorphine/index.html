Top Ranked Apomorphine ED Treatments

How apomorphine actually reaches men today — the formulations, how they're delivered, and how they stack up. Ranked by mechanism and delivery, not a substitute for a clinician's advice.

Apomorphine's chemistry dictates its delivery. Because it's broken down in the gut and liver, a swallowed pill barely works — so every serious ED formulation is sublingual, dissolving under the tongue for fast absorption straight to the bloodstream and brain.1 The options below differ mainly in what apomorphine is combined with.

1
Combination sublingual

Sublingual apomorphine + PDE5

Compounded troches pair apomorphine with sildenafil and/or tadalafil, targeting both the central (brain) and peripheral (blood-flow) sides of an erection at once — a "coordinated action" single-ingredient pills can't offer.2

Standalone sublingual

Apomorphine sublingual (2–4 mg)

The classic ED approach and the format used in the pivotal trials: a low 2–4 mg dose under the tongue, with erections typically inside 15–20 minutes given sexual stimulation.34

Historic branded tablets

Uprima / Ixense (apomorphine SL)

The first branded sublingual apomorphine tablets, approved in Europe in the early 2000s at 2 mg and 3 mg. Commercially withdrawn, but they set the dosing and safety profile still cited today.1

The pattern is simple: apomorphine brings the central mechanism, and modern formulas increasingly combine it with a peripheral PDE5 inhibitor rather than replace one with the other.

What to weigh when comparing options

  • Mechanism fit. A central agent makes most sense when arousal — not just blood flow — is the bottleneck.1
  • Dose & tolerability. Pooled trial data found 2 and 4 mg offered the most acceptable balance of effect and side effects, with nausea easing over the first weeks of use.4
  • Delivery. Sublingual is essential; swallowed apomorphine is largely inactivated before it works.2
  • Combination vs. standalone. Troches that add a PDE5 inhibitor aim to cover both pathways in one dose.2
Availability & safety. Apomorphine is FDA-approved in the U.S. only for Parkinson's disease; branded sublingual ED tablets are no longer marketed. Where used for ED it is typically compounded and prescribed off-label. It can lower blood pressure — use caution with antihypertensives and only under a clinician's supervision.3
